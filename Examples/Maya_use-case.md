# Example: “Maya”, product designer (dummy data)

*What the app looks like with writing that contains enough reasoning to analyze.*

Maya is a fictional composite — 34, living in Chicago, a senior product designer at a mid-sized software company, in a long-term relationship. She has just been offered her first people-management role.

The note below was typed in her phone around midnight after talking with her manager and partner. It has not been edited into an essay. Maya is trying to work out whether she wants the promotion, or only wanted to be offered it.

---

## Output

#### Highlight

Underneath all of this is a mind that treats clarity as something that must be *earned* through self-confrontation—but also suspects that any clarity it reaches might be a sophisticated rationalization. This creates real intellectual honesty and real paralysis in the same structure. The passage ends where it should: not with a resolution, but with permission. You're allowed to ask. The discovery wasn't what you want; it was that wanting is a legitimate thing to negotiate from.


#### How you may think


You think by testing formulations against your own resistance. A thought arrives—"I'm burned out"—and almost immediately you press on it: *that's not completely true because last night I spent two hours rebuilding the onboarding prototype.* This isn't contrarianism; it's a reflexive checking of statements against available evidence before letting them stand. You seem to distrust explanations that feel too clean or too convenient, especially when they're about yourself.

There's a signature move here: you'll state something, then introduce a counter-case, then try to find the narrower claim that survives both. "I'm not tired of work exactly. I'm tired of days where I talk about work all day and can't point to what changed because of me." The final formulation is more specific and harder to dismiss. You think toward precision by ruling things out.

You also think in dialogue with internalized voices—your dad's sentence about pulling back, Sam's observation about wanting to be chosen. These voices aren't authorities you defer to, but they aren't dismissed either. You argue with them, which means they have weight. Your thinking is populated; you're rarely just alone with a question.

#### How you may reason


Your reasoning relies heavily on cases and counter-cases. The law school decision, the marathon, Northstar, covering for Priya—these aren't digressions. They're your primary evidence. You build arguments the way a lawyer might build precedent, except you're the entire case history. When you want to know if a current pattern is fear or genuine preference, you look for past instances where the same pattern led somewhere you can now evaluate.

You're suspicious of reasoning that serves your comfort. "The part I still don't trust is whether I'm drawing that distinction because it's true or because it gives me an intelligent-sounding escape hatch." This is a meaningful check, but it also creates a bind: any conclusion that lets you off the hook becomes automatically suspect. Your reasoning has an asymmetry—conclusions that cost you something feel more trustworthy than conclusions that protect you.

You reason toward action only after you've exhausted self-interrogation. The practical tests you generate at the end—ask Elena, shadow Colin, propose a scoped experiment—arrive late, almost reluctantly, as if you needed to earn the right to act by first proving you've understood yourself.

#### How you may discover


You discover things by writing or speaking past the point where you already know what you think. The line "I may want leadership without people management" arrives with surprise in it: *which sounds obvious now that I've written it.* The discovery wasn't hidden; it was unassembled. You seem to find out what you know by watching what you say when you keep going.

Your discoveries often come through correcting your own word choices. "Vain isn't right. Being asked mattered because it proved they see me." The first word is wrong, and noticing it's wrong opens a door to something more accurate. You treat your own language as data, which means imprecision is productive—it gives you something to push against.

You also discover by noticing your own evasions. The passage ends with an explicit recognition: "I think that's the thing I was avoiding." You discovered the avoidance by circling the topic long enough to see what you kept not saying directly.

How you may learn


You learn from experience primarily when you can revisit it with a question you didn't have at the time. The Priya coverage, for instance, becomes legible evidence only now, in the context of this decision. You remembered being proud, but you *notice* the relief. The learning happens in the re-reading.

You also learn by metabolizing other people's framings—but slowly, and with friction. Sam's observation was "irritating," which didn't stop it from working on you. You pushed back, then refined, then partially accepted. You seem to learn from others best when you're allowed to resist first. Agreement that comes too easily might not stick.

There's a tension in how you learn about yourself: you collect evidence carefully, but you're hesitant to let it settle into conclusions. "I keep trying to turn this into a character verdict: ambitious or not ambitious, brave or avoidant." You recognize that these categories are too blunt, but I suspect you also resist them because a verdict would end the inquiry. You may learn more easily in motion than in summary.

## Input

I keep saying I’m deciding whether to take the manager role, but I think I’m mostly trying to find a version of the decision that doesn’t make me feel stupid.

Six months ago I wanted this. Or I said I wanted it. I was annoyed that Colin kept getting pulled into director meetings when half the time I was the one who had actually figured out the problem. So when Elena said today that they want me to take his team after he leaves, my first reaction should have been finally. Instead I felt my stomach drop and immediately started asking about timing, headcount, whether I could keep any design work. Very normal practical questions, except I already knew what answer I was hoping for: something that would make it impossible.

The easy explanation is I’m burned out. I am tired. But that’s not completely true because last night I spent two hours rebuilding the onboarding prototype after dinner and had to make myself stop. Nobody asked me to do that. I’m not tired of work exactly. I’m tired of days where I talk about work all day and can’t point to what changed because of me.

But then I hear how childish that sounds. Managers do change things, obviously. Elena changed my entire first year here by giving me room and protecting the work. So maybe I’m just scared I won’t be good at it. That’s probably part of it. Although when Priya was out last spring and I covered the team for three weeks, it went objectively well. We shipped. Nobody quit. Two people told Elena I gave clearer feedback than Priya. I remember being proud when she told me that, but mostly I remember the relief when Priya came back and I could close the staffing spreadsheet.

That seems like evidence, right? Not that I can’t manage. More like being able to do it is not the same as wanting the job. I keep collapsing those two things. If I’m good at something, declining it feels wasteful. Same with law school, actually. I never even wanted to be a lawyer by the end, but because I had gotten in, not going suddenly became “throwing away” all the work. I went for a year and then left, which was much more expensive than admitting the answer earlier.

I can already hear my dad saying that I always pull back right before things get serious. That sentence has followed me into everything. Sometimes it is true. I quit the marathon plan at week nine because I was afraid I’d be slow. I nearly didn’t submit my portfolio here because I thought it wasn’t senior enough. But I also stayed at Northstar two years longer than I should have because leaving would have looked like another example of me quitting. So “you pull back” can push me into doing things I don’t want just as easily as it can name fear.

Sam said something irritating tonight: maybe I wanted to be chosen for the role more than I wanted the role. I told him that was unfair because I have been asking for more scope for a year. But more scope is not automatically more direct reports. I want to decide what problem we are solving earlier. I want to be in the room when product makes the tradeoff. I want to coach newer designers when they’re stuck. I do not want half my week to become calibration, performance plans, hiring approvals, and explaining why someone’s promotion did not happen. At least I don’t think I do. The annoying part is that Sam may be right and I hated hearing it because it made the whole thing sound vain.

No, vain isn’t right. Being asked mattered because it proved they see me. That is different from wanting the actual Tuesday afternoon of the job.

I notice I keep trying to turn this into a character verdict: ambitious or not ambitious, brave or avoidant, leader or maker. Those are dramatic categories and none of them tell me what to do Monday. The actual evidence is more specific. I like mentoring when there is a real piece of work between us. I like setting direction when I know the material. I dislike being the emotional and administrative container for ten people. I may want leadership without people management, which sounds obvious now that I’ve written it, but at work those have been presented as the same ladder.

The part I still don’t trust is whether I’m drawing that distinction because it’s true or because it gives me an intelligent-sounding escape hatch. I could test it instead of solving my personality tonight. Ask Elena whether the staff role is real or just something they say may exist later. Ask to see what Colin actually spends his week doing, not the cleaned-up version. Maybe take the team for a month before the title is final, although that risks doing the job and making it harder to say no. Or ask to own Q4 product strategy and mentor Nina without taking all six reports. Her answer would tell me whether they want my leadership or simply need someone to absorb Colin’s workload.

I think that’s the thing I was avoiding. I was treating the offer as recognition, so negotiating the shape of it felt ungrateful. But an offer is also information about a need they have. I’m allowed to ask whether their need and the work I want are actually the same thing.
