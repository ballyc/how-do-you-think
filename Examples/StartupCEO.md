# Example: "Brian" (dummy data)

*What the Vercel app looks like in action using a fictional persona.*

Brian is 37 and lives in Brooklyn with his wife and four-year-old daughter. He is the co-founder and CEO of a B2B software company that automates operational work for logistics teams.

The company raised a $16 million Series A seven months ago. It has grown from 17 to 43 employees, has roughly $3.5 million in annual recurring revenue, and is trying to more than double that over the next year.

Brian recorded the following voice memo (input) in an Uber home after staying late to prepare for a board meeting. The transcript is intentionally left in its spoken form.

---

## Output

Highlight:

Underneath all of this is a specific engine: you learn by making your own behavior visible to yourself, then reasoning about it as if it were a system you inherited rather than one you chose. The shift from "I keep joining these calls" to "what does it mean that the company is structured so I join these calls" is the move that lets you see clearly. You become, briefly, an analyst of your own organization — and that analytical distance is where your hardest thinking happens.


How you may think


You think by holding multiple interpretations of the same situation simultaneously, then testing them against each other until one wins or you notice they're all partially true. The TransFleet/Pioneer passage is a clear example: you move from "Daniel should have asked" to "but this case is different" to "I never explained the rule" to "maybe there isn't a rule" to "which means the company cannot operate without me" — and then you immediately check that conclusion against counterevidence. This is not rambling. It's a structured move: assert, qualify, escalate, test.

You also think in systems more than in moments. When you describe your interventions, you spontaneously shift from evaluating "was my feedback useful?" to evaluating "the accumulated effect of forty useful interventions." That systems-level reframe comes naturally to you, but it seems to arrive *after* you've already been operating at the moment level for a while. You see the system once you've accumulated enough friction.

There's a signature move in your thinking: the self-interrupting correction. "No, that's too dramatic." "That sounds insane. Or maybe not insane, just not something founders say out loud." These aren't hedges — they're real-time calibrations. You're monitoring your own claims for exaggeration or self-deception and adjusting mid-thought.

How you may reason


Your reasoning depends heavily on counterexamples. When you make a claim — "nobody has enough context" — you immediately reach for a case that breaks it: "Elena is the obvious counterexample." This gives your thinking a characteristic shape: generalization, exception, revised generalization. You don't seem to trust a pattern until it survives contact with a case that could disprove it.

You also reason through opposition. "We need better people" vs. "I need to become a better CEO." "Brian handles it" vs. bureaucracy. You often frame decisions as two poles, then resist both, looking for what you call "something between." The pressure to find that middle space seems to be where your real reasoning happens — not at either extreme but in the tension.

One structural note: you distinguish sharply between being technically correct and being *actually true*. The board deck was "technically correct but not actually true." This suggests you reason in at least two registers — one about accuracy, one about meaning — and you don't let the first substitute for the second.

How you may discover


You discover by accumulating observations until they suddenly cohere into a sentence you hadn't planned to say. The key line — "I don't evaluate the accumulated effect of forty useful interventions" — reads like a discovery happening in real time. It arrives not through deduction but through enough examples piling up that the structure underneath becomes visible.

Your discoveries often have a confessional quality: "That one hurt a little." "That is probably the uglier answer." You seem to trust insights more when they implicate you in some way you hadn't admitted. Discoveries that let you off the hook get tested harder than discoveries that cost you something.

You also discover through thought experiments. "What would actually break if I disappeared for a week?" You answer your own question seriously, case by case, and then notice what the combined answer reveals about your real attachment. The discovery isn't in any single case — it's in the pattern of all of them being fine.

How you may learn


You learn by catching yourself in contradictions — not logical contradictions, but behavioral ones. "I tell them to own things, but I remain available for every decision." You list four of these in a row, and the learning seems to happen not in any one of them but in seeing them stacked. The structure of your own behavior becomes visible through repetition.

You learn slowly from abstract advice and quickly from cases. "Only work on what matters" bounces off you, but Elena's success lands. "Decision system" sounds like consultant language until you re-derive it from your own frustration with undefined normal. You seem to need to re-earn concepts through your own examples before they become usable.

There's a temporal pattern in your learning: you often don't absorb something until the same friction has occurred multiple times across different contexts. Six weeks of product stalling, multiple deals where context lived in your head, Tuesday one-on-ones that are really status updates — the lesson surfaces only when the instances accumulate.

## Input

Okay, I need to get this out somewhere because I have a seven-thirty with Rachel tomorrow and I know what she’s going to say. She’s going to say I need to protect my time, and then I’m going to agree, and then at eight-fifteen I’ll join the implementation call with Northline because they’re unhappy and apparently I’m the only person who can make them feel like we understand their business.

Which may actually be true. That’s the problem.

Today was ridiculous. I started with the enterprise pricing review, then jumped into the Northline escalation, then interviewed the VP Sales candidate, then rewrote half the board deck because the story was technically correct but not actually true, then product needed a decision on the routing feature, then Daniel asked whether he could offer TransFleet a discount, then someone sent me the new homepage and I spent forty minutes changing six words in the headline.

And I did not do the one thing I said was the most important thing for me to do today, which was figure out whether we are actually staying focused on logistics or quietly turning into a general workflow company because every large prospect asks us to automate something different.

I keep saying I need ten of me. I said it twice today, actually. Once as a joke and once not as a joke.

But ten of me doing what? Ten people joining every meeting late and changing the decision? Ten people answering Slack at midnight? That is not a company. That is just a more expensive version of my current problem.

The frustrating thing is there are completely valid reasons for me to be involved in all of these things.

Northline is our largest customer and I sold them personally. Their COO texts me. The routing feature changes the original product logic. The VP Sales hire could determine whether we hit the number. The board deck is how the board understands the company. Pricing matters. Positioning matters. All of it matters.

So “only work on what matters” is useless advice because everything that reaches me has already passed through three people who decided it mattered.

Maybe the real issue is that too many things can only be resolved with context that lives in my head.

But whose fault is that?

I say people need to use judgment. Then when they use judgment and I disagree, I come in with twelve pieces of history they did not know and reverse the decision. After that they ask me earlier next time. Which I interpret as them not taking enough ownership.

That’s not fair.

Daniel asked about the TransFleet discount because two months ago I got annoyed when he offered Pioneer twenty percent without checking with me. But Pioneer was different because they were already expanding and would probably have signed at full price. TransFleet is strategic because it gives us the Southeast and a reference for cold-chain operators. I can see why those are different. I’m not sure I’ve ever explained the rule that makes them different.

Maybe there isn’t a rule. Maybe I just know it when I see it, which is another way of saying the company cannot operate without me.

No, that’s too dramatic. The company operated while I was fundraising. Sort of.

Actually, did it? Product shipped almost nothing for six weeks because everyone was waiting for me to settle the enterprise roadmap. Sales kept moving because Elena basically took over. Customer success held things together. So parts of the company operated. The parts I had genuinely handed over operated.

That seems important.

Elena is the obvious counterexample to my whole “nobody has enough context” thing. She has enough context because I gave her the accounts and stopped interfering. And now she knows more about our customers than I do.

I should feel good about that. I do feel good about it. I also hate being on a customer call when they mention something she knows and I don’t. There’s a tiny moment where I feel like I’ve been demoted inside my own company.

That sounds insane.

Or maybe not insane, just not something founders say out loud.

For four years, being the person who knew everything was how I knew I was useful. In the beginning, there was no difference between the company succeeding and me personally solving the next problem. A customer had a weird integration, I figured it out. The demo broke, I fixed it. We needed the first ten customers, I found them. An employee was confused, I sat next to them.

Now I can spend an entire day working and the company is arguably worse because six people waited for my opinion and three people had to redo something after I gave it.

But the moments when I jump in and catch something are real too.

The team wanted to ship the new exception-handling flow last month. I looked at it for twenty minutes and found that it failed on the exact workflow our two biggest customers use. If I had stayed out because I was “empowering the team,” we would have damaged two accounts.

So what am I supposed to learn from that? That I need to be in every product review? That the team is not senior enough? That I haven’t taught them what to look for? All three could be true.

I keep going back and forth between “we need better people” and “I need to become a better CEO.”

“We need better people” is a very convenient conclusion because it preserves the idea that my involvement is necessary.

But we probably do need more senior people. We raised the Series A partly to build a leadership team. The board keeps asking when I’m hiring a real VP Product and a real revenue leader. I know what they mean by real, but I still hear it as: the people who got us here are no longer good enough, including me.

The VP Sales interview today was strange. He was clearly more experienced than I am at building a sales organization. He had a sensible answer for everything. Territories, forecasting, qualification, compensation, hiring sequence. I should have felt relieved.

Instead I kept looking for evidence that he didn’t really understand how our deals work.

He said founders should get out of normal sales calls after the playbook is repeatable, and my immediate thought was that he doesn’t understand that our playbook is not repeatable yet. Which is true.

But maybe it stays unrepeatable because I keep treating every deal as special.

That one hurt a little.

The board wants us to go from three and a half to eight million ARR. That means we cannot keep selling based on me remembering that a director at a prospect used to work with someone at Northline and therefore we should lead with yard operations instead of dispatch.

But that intuition is also why we win.

I don’t want to replace judgment with process and become one of those companies where everyone follows the CRM stages while the actual customer is obviously saying no.

There has to be something between “Brian handles it” and bureaucracy.

Rachel called it a decision system. I rolled my eyes because it sounded like consultant language. But maybe what she means is that people need to know which decisions they own, what good looks like, and when something is exceptional enough to bring to me.

Right now everything is exceptional because we have not defined normal.

I also have nine direct reports. Nine sounded manageable when five of them were people I had worked with since the beginning. It is not manageable now. My one-on-ones take most of Tuesday, and half of them are status updates because I’m afraid there’s something happening that I won’t know about until it is a problem.

Then I complain that nobody is thinking strategically.

They probably don’t have time to think strategically because they spend Tuesday explaining everything to me.

I’m tired, but I don’t think this is regular burnout. I still want to work. That is almost worse. I get home exhausted and then open the product at eleven because changing something in the product feels clean. There is a problem, I make a change, the change exists.

CEO work does not feel like that.

I have a conversation with someone. Maybe they make a better decision three weeks later. I hire someone and don’t know if it was a good decision for six months. I repeat the strategy and still find out two teams heard different things. There is no satisfying moment where I can point and say, I built that today.

Maybe I keep inserting myself into product and sales because those are places where I can still feel productive in the old way.

Meanwhile the things only I can do are sitting there. Decide the market. Hire the executives. Keep the board aligned. Make sure we do not spend sixteen million dollars hiring ahead of a strategy we haven’t actually chosen.

That last one scares me more than I admit.

Before the round, being stretched thin felt scrappy. After the round, the same behavior feels irresponsible. We told investors the money would let us build the machine. Seven months later, I am still a major part of the machine.

And everyone is working incredibly hard. I don’t want this to sound like the team is failing me. They are not. If anything, I think I am making it harder for them to become what I keep saying I need.

I tell them to own things, but I remain available for every decision.

I tell them speed matters, but I ask them to wait until I can review it.

I tell them I care about outcomes, then edit the method.

I tell them to challenge me, but I have four years of context and can usually win the argument.

Winning the argument is not necessarily the same as being right. Or even if I am right on that decision, maybe I am wrong at the company level because I just taught another person not to decide without me.

That might be the part I have been missing. I evaluate each intervention individually. Was my feedback useful? Did I improve the deal? Did I catch the product issue?

Usually the answer is yes.

I don’t evaluate the accumulated effect of forty useful interventions, which may be a team that waits for Brian.

That is uncomfortable because it means the bottleneck is not caused by my failures. It is partly caused by me being helpful.

Laura asked me last night whether things are supposed to calm down now that the fundraise is over. I told her this is just a particularly intense month. I have told her that every month since February.

Emma was asleep when I left and asleep when I got home three days this week. I’m not including that to make this into some dramatic founder sacrifice thing. I chose this. Laura chose it too, within reason. And I like the work. I love the company.

But I don’t think loving the company means I should personally experience every part of it.

What would actually break if I disappeared for a week?

Northline would probably be fine. Elena would handle it.

Product might make one decision I dislike, but it would not destroy the company.

The board deck would get done. It might use too many words, but it would get done.

The VP Sales process would continue.

People might even resolve things faster because waiting for me would not be an option.

So why does the idea of disappearing for a week make me nervous?

Partly because something could go wrong.

Partly because nothing could go wrong.

That is probably the uglier answer.

I keep saying I need ten Brians, but maybe what I actually need is a company that does not require Brian in ten places. And I don’t yet know how to build that without feeling like I am slowly removing the thing that made the company work.

Maybe the job now is not to clone my judgment. It is to make my judgment visible enough that other people can improve it.

I don’t know. That sounds good in a voice note. Tomorrow Northline will call and I’ll probably join.

But I should at least notice when I do it. Am I joining because this genuinely requires the CEO, because I have context nobody else has, or because being needed is the easiest way for me to know I’m doing my job?

Those are not the same thing.
